With tools like Claude, Gemini, Cursor, and GitHub Copilot reshaping how code gets written, interviewers want to know if you're thoughtful about the industry shift — not in denial, and not panicking.

## Examples

**Input:** "With the rise of AI coding tools like Claude, Copilot, and Cursor — do you think they can replace frontend engineers? How do you see your career evolving?"
**Output:** A nuanced, self-aware answer that shows you actively use these tools and understand both their power and their limits.

## Solution

```text
Framework: Acknowledge → Differentiate → Adapt → Grow

"This is something I think about a lot — and I've been actively
using these tools, so my answer comes from hands-on experience,
not speculation.

ACKNOWLEDGE — AI IS A REAL SHIFT:
Let's be honest: AI tools like Claude, Cursor, and Copilot have
dramatically changed my workflow. I use them daily. They're
incredible at generating boilerplate, writing tests, explaining
unfamiliar code, and even prototyping entire features. Tasks that
took me hours now take minutes. Anyone who dismisses this shift
isn't paying attention.

DIFFERENTIATE — WHAT AI CAN'T DO (YET):
But after months of heavy usage, I've also seen clear limits:
- AI struggles with ambiguous product requirements. It can't sit
  in a meeting, read between the lines, and figure out what the
  PM actually needs vs. what they said.
- It doesn't understand your users. I've seen AI generate
  technically correct code that's a terrible UX decision.
- Complex system design — like deciding how to structure state
  management across a large app, or making trade-offs between
  performance and maintainability — still requires human judgment.
- Debugging production issues that span multiple services, involve
  race conditions, or depend on understanding business context.

The pattern I see: AI replaces tasks, not roles. It replaces the
'typing' part of engineering, but engineering was never mostly
about typing.

ADAPT — HOW I USE AI AS A MULTIPLIER:
I've restructured my workflow around AI:
- I use Cursor/Claude for first drafts, then I review and refine.
  I'm basically a senior engineer reviewing a fast but junior
  developer's code all day.
- I use AI to explore approaches I wouldn't have time to try
  manually — 'show me 3 ways to implement this' — then I pick
  the best one using my judgment.
- For code reviews, I run AI analysis first, then focus my human
  attention on architecture and business logic concerns.

This has made me roughly 2-3x more productive on implementation,
which means I spend more time on the high-value work: talking to
users, designing systems, mentoring, and making product decisions.

GROW — WHERE I'M INVESTING:
I'm deliberately growing in areas that AI amplifies rather than
replaces:
- System design and architecture — AI needs someone to tell it
  what to build
- Product sense and user empathy — understanding the 'why'
- Communication and cross-team collaboration
- AI-assisted development skills themselves — prompt engineering,
  knowing when to trust AI and when to verify
- Deep debugging and performance optimization

I see AI like the shift from assembly to high-level languages.
It didn't eliminate programmers — it made them more powerful and
raised the bar for what we're expected to deliver."
```

## Explanation

**What makes a strong answer:**
1. **You actually use the tools** — not theorizing from the sidelines
2. **You're honest about the impact** — no denial or downplaying
3. **You show nuance** — you understand both capabilities and limits
4. **You have a concrete adaptation plan** — not vague optimism
5. **You frame it as opportunity** — AI makes you more valuable, not less

**What interviewers are really assessing:**
- **Adaptability** — can you evolve with the industry?
- **Self-awareness** — do you understand where your real value lies?
- **Growth mindset** — are you threatened or energized by change?
- **Practical experience** — have you actually used these tools, or just read about them?
- **Strategic thinking** — can you see where the industry is heading?

**Red flags to avoid:**
- "AI can't write real code" — this is denial and shows you haven't used modern tools
- "We're all going to be replaced" — doom without a plan is not a good look
- "I don't really use AI tools" — this signals you're falling behind
- Being overly technical without addressing the human/product side
