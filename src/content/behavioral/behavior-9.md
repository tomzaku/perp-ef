Companies want engineers who elevate the entire team, not just themselves. This question tests your ability to teach, communicate, and invest in others' growth.

## Examples

**Input:** "Tell me about a time you mentored or helped develop someone."
**Output:** A story showing patience, tailored guidance, and measurable growth in the other person.

## Solution

```text
Framework: STAR with focus on the mentee's growth

SITUATION: "A junior engineer joined our team fresh out of
bootcamp. They had solid JavaScript fundamentals but no
experience with React, TypeScript, or our codebase — which
had 400+ components and complex state management."

TASK: "I volunteered to be their onboarding buddy. My goal
was to get them contributing independently within 6 weeks."

ACTION: "Instead of just assigning docs to read, I created a
structured learning path:

Week 1-2: PAIRED PROGRAMMING
I picked small, well-scoped tickets and we coded together. I
explained my thought process out loud — why I chose certain
patterns, how I navigated the codebase, how I debugged issues.

Week 3-4: REVERSE PAIRING
They drove the keyboard while I observed. I'd ask guiding
questions instead of giving answers: 'What do you think would
happen if...' and 'Where would you look for...'

Week 5-6: REVIEW AND SUPPORT
They worked independently. I did thorough, encouraging code
reviews — for every piece of constructive feedback, I'd include
something they did well.

Throughout, we had weekly 1-on-1s where I asked what was
confusing, not what they'd learned. The confusion questions
surfaced gaps that 'what did you learn' never would."

RESULT: "By week 5, they were shipping features independently.
Within 3 months, they were one of our most reliable contributors
on the accessibility improvements project. They later told me
the 'reverse pairing' technique was what made the biggest
difference — it forced them to build their own mental model
instead of copying mine.

I've since used this 3-phase approach with two more new hires."
```

## Explanation

**Effective mentorship principles:**
1. **Meet them where they are** — assess their level first
2. **Show your thinking, not just answers** — model the process
3. **Gradually release control** — pair → reverse pair → independent
4. **Ask questions instead of lecturing** — Socratic method works
5. **Celebrate progress** — confidence matters as much as skill

**What interviewers want to see:**
- You **voluntarily** invest in others (not just when assigned)
- You can **adapt your communication** to different skill levels
- The mentee had **measurable growth** (not just "they got better")
- You **learned something** from the experience too
